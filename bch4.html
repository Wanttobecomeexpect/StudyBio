Morphology of Flowering Plants

Study of external form and structure of flowering (angiosperm) plants: root, stem, leaf, flower, fruit, seed.

Introduction

This chapter deals with the morphology ‚Äî external form and structure ‚Äî of flowering plants. It helps to identify plant parts and learn their variations and modifications. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

The Root

Roots anchor the plant in soil and absorb water and minerals. There are two main root systems: tap-root (common in dicots) and fibrous root (common in monocots). :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Roots can be modified for special functions (storage roots like turnip, prop roots in banyan, pneumatophores in mangroves, etc.). :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

The Stem

The stem supports leaves and flowers, conducts water, minerals and food, and in many plants stores food. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Stems may be modified: underground (rhizome, corm, tuber, bulb), aerial (tendrils, thorns), or for storage and vegetative propagation. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

The Leaf

Leaves are lateral, generally flattened organs borne on stem. They are main photosynthetic organs in most plants. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Leaf-types: simple (single lamina), compound (lamina divided into leaflets ‚Äî pinnate or palmate). Venation pattern: reticulate in dicots, parallel in monocots. Phyllotaxy (leaf arrangement on stem): alternate, opposite, whorled. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Leaves may also show modifications (tendrils, spines, storage leaves) depending on habitat / function. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Inflorescence

Inflorescence refers to arrangement of flowers on the floral axis. Two main types in flowering plants: racemose (indeterminate) and cymose (determinate). :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

The Flower

A flower is a reproductive structure, typically organized in four whorls: calyx (sepals), corolla (petals), androecium (stamens ‚Äî male), gynoecium (carpels ‚Äî female). :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

Flowers vary in symmetry (actinomorphic vs zygomorphic), fusion of parts, and ovary position (superior/inferior) ‚Äî features important in classification. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

The Fruit

Fruit develops from fertilized ovary; pericarp (fruit wall) encloses seeds. Fruits may be dry or fleshy. :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

Fruits may be simple, aggregate or multiple (composite). Example ‚Äî drupe, berry, legume etc. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

Seeds inside fruits are dispersed by various agents (wind, water, animals) depending on fruit/seed traits. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

The Seed

Seed is formed from fertilised ovule. It consists of seed-coat (testa + tegmen), embryo (embryonic axis + cotyledons), and in many cases endosperm (food reserve). :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

Differences between monocot and dicot seeds: dicots have two cotyledons; monocots have one cotyledon and often a large endosperm. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

Seeds ensure reproduction and dispersal; their form and structure reflect adaptation to environment. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}

Summary
