Morphology of Flowering Plants

Study of external form and structure of flowering (angiosperm) plants: root, stem, leaf, flower, fruit, seed.

Introduction

This chapter deals with the morphology ‚Äî external form and structure ‚Äî of flowering plants. It helps to identify plant parts and learn their variations and modifications.

Aspect Description
Morphology Study of external form and structure of plants.
Importance Helps in plant identification and classification.

The Root

Roots anchor the plant in soil and absorb water and minerals. There are two main root systems: tap-root (common in dicots) and fibrous root (common in monocots).

Roots can be modified for special functions (storage roots like turnip, prop roots in banyan, pneumatophores in mangroves, etc.).

Root Type Example
Tap Root Carrot, Radish
Fibrous Root Wheat, Grass
Modified Roots Turnip (Storage), Banyan (Prop)

The Stem

The stem supports leaves and flowers, conducts water, minerals and food, and in many plants stores food.

Stems may be modified: underground (rhizome, corm, tuber, bulb), aerial (tendrils, thorns), or for storage and vegetative propagation.

Stem Modification Example
Underground Potato (Tuber), Ginger (Rhizome)
Aerial Grapevine (Tendrils), Citrus (Thorns)

The Leaf

Leaves are lateral, generally flattened organs borne on stem. They are main photosynthetic organs in most plants.

Leaf-types: simple (single lamina), compound (lamina divided into leaflets ‚Äî pinnate or palmate). Venation pattern: reticulate in dicots, parallel in monocots. Phyllotaxy (leaf arrangement on stem): alternate, opposite, whorled.

Leaves may also show modifications (tendrils, spines, storage leaves) depending on habitat / function.

Leaf Type Example
Simple Mango
Compound Neem (Pinnate), Silk Cotton (Palmate)
Modified Pea (Tendrils), Cactus (Spines)

Inflorescence

Inflorescence refers to arrangement of flowers on the floral axis. Two main types in flowering plants: racemose (indeterminate) and cymose (determinate).

Type Characteristic
Racemose Flowers bloom in acropetal succession.
Cymose Flowers bloom in basipetal succession.

The Flower

A flower is a reproductive structure, typically organized in four whorls: calyx (sepals), corolla (petals), androecium (stamens ‚Äî male), gynoecium (carpels ‚Äî female).

Flowers vary in symmetry (actinomorphic vs zygomorphic), fusion of parts, and ovary position (superior/inferior) ‚Äî features important in classification.

Feature Example
Actinomorphic Mustard
Zygomorphic Pea
Superior Ovary China Rose
Inferior Ovary Guava

The Fruit

Fruit develops from fertilized ovary; pericarp (fruit wall) encloses seeds. Fruits may be dry or fleshy.

Fruits may be simple, aggregate or multiple (composite). Example ‚Äî drupe, berry, legume etc. Seeds inside fruits are dispersed by various agents (wind, water, animals) depending on fruit/seed traits.

Fruit Type Example
Simple Mango
Aggregate Custard Apple
Multiple Pineapple

The Seed

Seed is formed from fertilised ovule. It consists of seed-coat (testa + tegmen), embryo (embryonic axis + cotyledons), and in many cases endosperm (food reserve).

Differences between monocot and dicot seeds: dicots have two cotyledons; monocots have one cotyledon and often a large endosperm. Seeds ensure reproduction and dispersal; their form and structure reflect adaptation to environment.

Seed Type Example
Dicot Bean
Monocot Maize

Summary

Understanding the morphology of flowering plants helps in identifying, classifying and studying diversity among angiosperms. Knowledge of modifications, structure, variation and adaptation is essential for taxonomy and practical botany.
