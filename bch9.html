Respiration in Plants

How plants oxidise organic molecules to release energy (ATP) for metabolic activities.

Introduction

Respiration is the biochemical process in which cells break down glucose to release energy in the form of ATP. This energy is essential for various metabolic activities. The process involves the oxidation of organic molecules, with carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ) and water (H‚ÇÇO) as by-products in aerobic respiration.

Types of Respiration

Respiration can be classified into two types based on the presence or absence of oxygen:

  • Aerobic Respiration: Requires oxygen, produces a high yield of ATP.
  • Anaerobic Respiration: Occurs in the absence of oxygen, yields less ATP and produces ethanol or lactic acid.

Glycolysis

Glycolysis is the first step in cellular respiration, occurring in the cytosol. It involves the breakdown of one glucose molecule into two pyruvate molecules, producing a small amount of ATP and NADH.

Factors Affecting Respiration

Several factors influence the rate of respiration in plants, including temperature, oxygen availability, substrate concentration, and the presence of inhibitors.

Factor Effect
Temperature Increases respiration rate up to an optimum, then decreases due to enzyme denaturation.
Oxygen Availability Essential for aerobic respiration; limited oxygen shifts to anaerobic pathways.
Substrate Concentration Higher substrate levels increase respiration rate until saturation.
Respiratory Inhibitors Inhibit enzymes in the respiratory pathway, reducing ATP production.

Summary

Respiration is a vital process that provides energy in the form of ATP. The pathways of glycolysis, the Krebs Cycle, and the ETC explain the energy yield and intermediates for biosynthesis. Anaerobic respiration serves as an alternative under oxygen-limited conditions.
