Biomolecules

Core classes of biological molecules, their structure and functional roles in living systems.

Introduction

Biomolecules are organic molecules that perform structural, catalytic, storage and information roles in living organisms. Major classes: water, carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, vitamins and minerals.

Water ‚Äî Properties & Importance

Water is polar, has high specific heat, cohesion, adhesion, solvent properties ‚Äî supports biochemical reactions, transport and thermal buffering in organisms.

Carbohydrates

Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose), disaccharides (sucrose), polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose). Functions: energy storage, structural (cellulose), recognition (glycoproteins).

Lipids

Lipids include fats, phospholipids, steroids. Hydrophobic molecules used for long-term energy storage, membrane structure (phospholipid bilayer) and signalling (steroid hormones).

Proteins

Composed of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. Structure levels: primary ‚Üí secondary ‚Üí tertiary ‚Üí quaternary. Roles: enzymes, structural proteins (collagen), transport (hemoglobin), defence (antibodies).

Enzymes (Basics)

Biological catalysts ‚Äî lower activation energy, highly specific. Have active sites; activity affected by pH, temperature, substrate concentration and inhibitors. Cofactors/coenzymes often required.

Nucleic Acids

DNA and RNA: polymers of nucleotides (sugar + phosphate + base). DNA stores genetic information; RNA involved in expression (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA). Complementary base pairing (A-T/U, G-C).

Vitamins & Minerals

Micronutrients with essential roles: vitamins often act as coenzymes (B group, C), minerals (Fe, Ca, Mg) are cofactors, structural elements and osmotic regulators.

Summary

Understanding structure and properties of biomolecules clarifies how cells carry out metabolism, growth and information transfer. These molecules form biochemical pathways studied later in physiology and biochemistry.
