Introduction & Importance

Photosynthesis is a fundamental biological process where plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. This process produces organic molecules and oxygen from carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ) and water (H‚ÇÇO), forming the basis of food chains and oxygen supply on Earth.

Aspect Details
Process Conversion of light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose
Importance Supports life by providing food and oxygen, maintaining the Earth's atmosphere

Site of Photosynthesis (Chloroplasts)

Photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts, specialized organelles in plant cells. Chloroplasts have a double membrane and contain structures like thylakoids (stacked into grana) and stroma. Light reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes, while the Calvin cycle takes place in the stroma.

Chloroplast Part Function
Thylakoids Site of light-dependent reactions, where ATP and NADPH are generated
Stroma Site of the Calvin cycle, where CO‚ÇÇ is fixed into carbohydrates

Photosynthetic Pigments

Photosynthetic pigments absorb light energy at specific wavelengths. Chlorophyll a is the primary pigment, while chlorophyll b and carotenoids act as accessory pigments, broadening the range of light absorption and protecting against photooxidation.

Pigment Role
Chlorophyll a Main pigment for light absorption and energy transfer
Chlorophyll b Accessory pigment that expands the range of light absorption
Carotenoids Protect against oxidative damage and absorb additional light

Light & Dark Reactions

Photosynthesis consists of two main stages: light-dependent reactions and light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle). Light reactions involve the photolysis of water, electron transport chain, and synthesis of ATP and NADPH. The Calvin cycle uses these products to fix CO‚ÇÇ into sugars.

Reaction Details
Light Reactions Occur in thylakoids; generate ATP and NADPH, release O‚ÇÇ
Dark Reactions Occur in stroma; use ATP and NADPH to fix CO‚ÇÇ into carbohydrates

Carbon Fixation (Calvin Cycle)

The Calvin cycle is a series of biochemical reactions that occur in the stroma. It consists of three phases: carboxylation, reduction, and regeneration. The enzyme Rubisco catalyzes the fixation of CO‚ÇÇ, which is central to photosynthetic carbon assimilation.

Phase Description
Carboxylation CO‚ÇÇ combines with RuBP to form 3-PGA
Reduction 3-PGA is reduced to G3P using ATP and NADPH
Regeneration RuBP is regenerated to continue the cycle

Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

Several environmental factors influence the rate of photosynthesis, including light intensity, CO‚ÇÇ concentration, temperature, and water availability. The concept of limiting factors explains how these variables interact to affect the process.

Factor Effect
Light Intensity Increases the rate up to a saturation point
CO‚ÇÇ Concentration Higher levels enhance the rate until other factors become limiting
Temperature Optimal range required; extreme temperatures reduce efficiency

Summary

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in carbohydrates. Understanding the light and dark reactions, as well as the factors affecting photosynthesis, provides insights into plant productivity and ecological balance.
